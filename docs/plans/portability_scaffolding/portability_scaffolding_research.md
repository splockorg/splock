## 1. Current State of LLM Routing

**The field has a stable taxonomy, and Splock's proposed design occupies a named, legitimate cell in it — the cheapest one.** The April-2026 survey by Moslem & Kelleher [1] organizes routing along a when/what/how framework — *when* the decision is made (pre-generation / post-generation / multi-stage), *what* signals feed it, and *how* it is computed (heuristic threshold rules and cost-based heuristics / supervised classifiers / bandits / RL policies) — crossed with six paradigms (difficulty-aware, human-preference-aligned, clustering-based, RL, uncertainty-based, cascading). The survey states that routing overhead "must be negligible," and its full text states that "simple threshold rules or cost-based heuristics require no training and can be applied directly at inference time." The earlier 2025 survey [2] splits the same territory into similarity-based / supervised / RL-based / generative methods, and pre- vs post-generation timing, noting post-generation methods "can incur higher latency and cost, since multiple LLMs may need to generate answers for a single query." Splock's `StaticRouter` → future `RuleRouter` sits in the {pre-generation, query-metadata signal, heuristic-rule} cell — the only cell whose decision is a pure in-process lookup.

**The three practitioner senses of "routing" are distinct problems:**

- **Proxy-layer routing** (LiteLLM Router [36], OpenRouter provider routing [56]) is *deployment-level* selection — the same logical model served by multiple providers/deployments, with fallbacks, cooldowns, and latency/price weighting. LiteLLM's default simple-shuffle strategy is documented as "Recommended for Production - Best performance with minimal latency overhead," while its Redis-backed usage-based strategies carry an explicit "not recommended for production due to performance impacts" warning. OpenRouter layers a separate cross-model product on top: its Auto Router (NotDiamond-powered, no additional fee) selects from a curated frontier pool and — critically — pins "the model selection — not just the provider" per session (5-minute inactivity timeout) explicitly to preserve conversation consistency and prompt-cache hits [57].
- **Semantic routing** (Aurelio's `semantic-router` [34]) matches a query embedding against named routes defined by example utterances. It is alive (v0.1.15, 2026-05-23; 3.7k stars) and deliberately positioned as a "superfast decision-making layer" that decides without waiting for LLM generation — but every decision costs one encoder inference (~100 ms-class typical with a local encoder per secondary sources [84]; sub-10 ms only in specific local-model demos; a network round-trip with remote encoders).
- **Classifier/predictive routing** predicts which model will succeed. The canonical line is RouteLLM (LMSYS, ICLR 2025) [3][5][6]: matrix-factorization/BERT/causal-LM routers trained on human preference data, cutting cost >2x (up to 85% on MT Bench at 95% GPT-4 performance) for a binary strong/weak pair. Successors target generalization to unseen models: GraphRouter (heterogeneous graph, ≥9.5% boost on new LLMs without retraining) [18], MixLLM (NAACL 2025; latency inside the reward) [19], and LMSYS's Prompt-to-Leaderboard (an LLM emitting per-prompt Bradley-Terry coefficients; its router hit #1 on Chatbot Arena in January 2025) [20]. Arch-Router (Katanemo, Jun 2025) [8][9] is the most Splock-relevant turn: routing by a *human-authored* Domain-Action policy config mapped to models — adding new models "without requiring retraining or architectural modifications" — though its matcher is a 1.5B LM (~50 ms p50). Cascade routing (ETH Zürich) [17] is the theoretically optimal unification of routing and cascading but is structurally post-generation and multi-stage — the expensive end.

**The latency-overhead spectrum** (synthesis; order-of-magnitude bands — the survey concedes systematic cross-class latency data is thin, though RouterArena [14] now publishes measured per-router routing latency as a leaderboard axis): rule/dict lookup ≈ 0 (µs, in-process) < local embedding match (single-digit-to-tens of ms; the survey's "negligible" exemplar GreenServ runs <8 ms/query) < small-classifier/small-LM inference (tens-to-hundreds of ms; Arch-Router ~50 ms p50; vLLM Semantic Router's *post-98×-optimization* long-prompt path still ~108 ms [31]) < LLM-judge/P2L/cascades (hundreds of ms to full extra generations). **Only rule lookup strictly satisfies "near-zero latency overhead."**

**Evaluation has matured past accuracy:** RouterBench [13] (405,467 precomputed outcomes; 11 models, 8 datasets/64 tasks) defined router quality as position on a cost-quality frontier (AIQ). RouterArena [14] scores six axes including routing latency and concludes "No router ranks at the top across all metrics" (GPT-5 ranked #7 on its accuracy-cost leaderboard). LLMRouterBench [16] (400K+ instances, 33 models) finds some commercial routers underperform simpler baselines. All major benchmarks evaluate single-turn routing; no 2026 work evaluating agentic-session routing was found.

**Two structural shifts matter for Splock:** (a) the open-source center of gravity has moved from libraries to *infrastructure gateways* — vLLM Semantic Router (5k stars; Go/Rust, ModernBERT classifiers, Envoy ExtProc, Kubernetes/Helm; backed by Red Hat/IBM/AMD) [46][47][48][50] — i.e., exactly the weight class the operator prohibits; and (b) providers now ship difficulty routing natively — GPT-5 launched (2025-08-07) as a unified system behind a real-time router, whose rocky launch and partial retreat (Auto/Fast/Thinking picker restored within days [73][74]; automatic routing paused for Free/Go tiers in Dec 2025 [75]) shows per-query difficulty routing is unstable product ground even for providers with full telemetry. **Gap confirmed:** routing-under-cache-affinity is nearly absent from academic literature (the 2026 survey contains nothing on prompt caching or price changes); OpenRouter's session-stickiness docs are the only substantive treatment [57].

## 2. Lightweight Frameworks

Every genuinely maintained candidate fails at least one hard constraint. Verdicts, verified against primary sources 2026-07-17:

| Candidate | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **semantic-router** (Aurelio) [34] | FAIL as dependency; best *shape* to copy | In-process, decision-only (returns a route name, never executes the call) — the right architecture. But: encoder inference in every decision path (~100 ms-class); and a disqualifying dep tree (`litellm>=1.83.7`, `openai`, `aiohttp`, `tornado`, `tiktoken`, `pydantic`, `numpy` as *core* deps). The litellm floor traces to CVE-2026-42208, a CVSS 9.8 pre-auth SQL injection in LiteLLM Proxy [35] — a critical supply-chain event inherited through a dep it needs only as a client. It also solves intent *inference* from free text, which Splock does not need. |
| **RouteLLM** (LMSYS) [6][7] | FAIL — stale + wrong problem | Last commit 2024-08-10; last PyPI release v0.2.0 2024-07-08; zero GitHub releases. `Controller` fuses decision to an OpenAI-compatible HTTP completion surface; `mf`/`sw_ranking` routers require an OpenAI API key for embeddings per decision. Solves binary strong-vs-weak cost/quality, not N-family policy routing. |
| **LiteLLM Router** (library mode) [36][38] | FAIL as import; **best design reference** (→ §3) | Genuinely in-process, deterministic, config-driven, very actively maintained (v1.92.0, 2026-07-12). But it is deployment-level load balancing, not intent routing — and `import litellm` alone costs ~1–1.1 s typical (21 s worst-case on some Macs), with both import-speed issues **closed as not planned** upstream [39][40]. Real overhead for Splock's short-lived `bin/*` processes. |
| **LLMRouter** (ulab-uiuc) [44] | FAIL — research workbench | 16+ *trainable* routers (KNN/SVM/MLP/MF/Elo/graph/causal-LM/…), Longformer embeddings, torch+transformers deps, training CLI, OpenAI-compatible proxy deployment. Its own README states the `llm` strategy makes two upstream calls and recommends `random`/`round_robin` for lower latency. |
| **vLLM Semantic Router / Plano (ex-archgw) / Envoy AI Gateway / K8s Inference Extension** [46][10][70][71] | FAIL — prohibited weight class | Sidecar/gateway data planes (Envoy ExtProc, CRDs, Helm) with classifier or LM matchers. Trend is *heavier*: Katanemo's matcher grew 1.5B → 4B; vLLM SR grew from one ModernBERT classifier to a six-signal decision engine. |
| **Agent-CLI proxies** (claude-code-router 35.9k★, routatic/proxy, claude-proxy) [51][52][53] | Not applicable | The community's entire practice for re-routing agent CLIs is a local HTTP proxy at the API boundary translating provider dialects. All are HTTP-boundary-only; none host spawned-CLI transports. No off-the-shelf equivalent of Splock's Tier-A `TransportRouter` over CLI-wrapping `ModelTransport`s exists. |
| **aisuite / any-llm** (unified-API shims) [54][55] | Not applicable | Provider-string registries over HTTP adapters with API-key auth; neither supports subscription-authenticated CLI harnesses, an agent-spawn tier, or per-dialect schema sanitizers. |

**Two calibration datapoints.** IBM Research (HF blog, 2026-07-15) [83] reports a full systems-optimization router costing ~6 ms and 2 kB per task — establishing that even sophisticated routing is latency-trivial *at decision time*; the authors argue the hard part is that "routing isn't really about choosing models. It's about optimizing systems" (their example: Sonnet's lower cache-read pricing outweighing higher base price — cost is a model×workload×infrastructure interaction). And the practitioner literature's "semantic-first" mainstream advice (e.g., Google Cloud's guide recommending semantic routing as the default for dynamic real-time routing [81]) applies to *free-text intent inference* — a problem Splock sits entirely upstream of, since `RouteQuery.intent`/`subjects` arrive pre-classified from the slash-command layer. Within the practitioner literature's own terms, Outcome School's condition [82] — rule-based is "very fast and very cheap," and "for a small system with a few clear query types, rule-based is enough" (stated in the source's terms) — is exactly Splock's situation. Note: do not claim a practitioner "rules-first consensus"; the strongest practitioner source recommends semantic routing for free-text — the correct argument is the applicability boundary (declared intent removes the only problem semantic/learned routers exist to solve).

**Bottom line:** no drop-in library fits the constraints. The only artifacts worth borrowing are *shapes*: semantic-router's decision-object-decoupled-from-execution API and Arch-Router's declarative policy-to-model config — both already mirrored by the qna's `RouteQuery`/`RouteDecision`/`routing_rules_v1` design.

## 3. The "North Star" Open-Source Tool

**Recommendation: model Splock's routing partially after LiteLLM's in-process `Router` class [36] — as a design reference only, never as a dependency — with the Arch-Router paper's config-policy schema shape [8] as the secondary influence on `routing_rules_v1.schema.json`.**

Why LiteLLM Router beats each alternative under Splock's constraints (all sub-claims verified 2026-07-17):

1. **Near-zero latency.** Its decision path is dict lookup + list filter (simple-shuffle default, documented "Best performance with minimal latency overhead"). It is the *only* battle-tested candidate in `StaticRouter`'s latency class: semantic-router needs an encoder (~100 ms-class), RouteLLM/LLMRouter need trained-model inference plus embeddings, Arch-Router/Plano needs a 1.5B (now 4B) LM at ~50 ms p50.
2. **Architecturally a library first.** The routing decision is separable from transport execution — exactly what Splock needs when the "transport" is a subprocess spawn (`claude` SDK, `codex exec`, Antigravity CLI), not an HTTP upstream. Every other maintained router is a proxy/gateway.
3. **Structural isomorphism with the qna design.** `model_list` deployments grouped under a `model_name` alias with `litellm_params` + `model_info` metadata ≈ family-keyed transport registry + catalogs; `enable_pre_call_checks=True` filtering deployments on declared facts before selection ≈ `required_capabilities <= transport.capabilities()`; `order` priority tiers + always-valid default ≈ first-match-wins rules with `static-default`; `CustomRoutingStrategyBase` as a single pluggable strategy seam ≈ `TransportRouter` ABC; chosen deployment exposed in `_hidden_params['model_id']` ≈ `RouteDecision.rule_id`/`resolved_model` forensics. This is proof the filter-then-select, alias-indirected design works at 53.9k-star production scale.
4. **Health.** The only candidate simultaneously active (v1.92.0, 2026-07-12; 1,404 releases), massive, and permissively licensed (MIT core). RouteLLM is dormant since 2024-08-10; LLMRouter is a ~7-month-old research harness; semantic-router is healthy but decision-model-mismatched.

**Patterns to copy:** the two-stage route (capability/eligibility filter → strategy pick, as distinct testable stages); alias-keyed registry with structured per-deployment metadata; the strategy as one pluggable ABC seam; ordered fallback config with an always-valid default; decision logging with the reason attached (merge with the "route receipts" proposal [21] — a compact record of model selection, tiers, and fallback rules per request; note: single-author preprint, treat as design inspiration, not a standard).

**Patterns to deliberately skip:** the provider mega-shim and its ~1 s import cost (closed as not planned upstream — hardening "model after, never import" into policy); the proxy server and everything proxy-only; Redis-backed usage/latency/least-busy strategies (stateful, carrying LiteLLM's own not-for-production warning, meaningless for spawned CLI sessions); cost-map tracking and retry machinery (Splock's retry loop and `OVERNIGHT_*` pin precedence already own those); cooldown state machines (a per-call fallback to `static-default` suffices for Tier-A batch calls).

**Why not the others, in one line each:** `semantic-router` — right philosophy (fast, in-process, deliberately not-an-orchestrator), wrong decision substrate (embeddings recover a signal Splock already has losslessly); `RouteLLM` — a ~2-year-stale artifact of pre-caching, pre-price-drop economics (see §5) solving a binary problem Splock doesn't have; `LLMRouter` — a torch-dependent research harness; vLLM SR / Plano — prohibited gateway class, and both are getting *heavier*, confirming no lightweight drop-in has emerged. Two 2026 developments discovered during verification reinforce the recommendation rather than undermine it.

## 4. Alignment & Critique of the qna Design

The qna artifact was re-read in full on 2026-07-17; every element below references its text directly. Verdict key: **ALIGNED** (matches state-of-the-art practice), **REINVENTION RISK** (an existing tool already does this in adoptable form), **DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED** (deliberately departs from mainstream practice, with good reason).

| qna element | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Two-tier (Tier A programmatic / Tier B interactive) routing analysis | ALIGNED |
| `RouteQuery`/`RouteDecision` frozen dataclasses, metadata-only | ALIGNED; the no-prompt-text choice DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED |
| `TransportRouter` ABC + `StaticRouter`-only Phase 0 | ALIGNED |
| Keyed transport registry (Part 1 §1) | ALIGNED (pattern); build-vs-adopt DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED |
| `capabilities()` frozenset gating (Part 1 §3) | ALIGNED, with a governance caveat |
| `host` vs `family` ClassVar split (Part 1 §2) | ALIGNED |
| Family-only decision; catalog + `OVERNIGHT_*` pins resolve model (Part 1 §4) | ALIGNED — strongly |
| Ordered first-match-wins declarative rules (Part 1 §5) | ALIGNED, with schema gaps to close |
| Agent Skills `metadata` frontmatter routing hints (Part 3) | DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED |
| Roster `schema_version: 3` pins + fixed-verifier invariant (Part 3) | ALIGNED / DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED (nuance below) |
| Intent-registry `subjects` + forensics fields (Part 2) | ALIGNED |

**Per-element critique:**

- **Two-tier analysis (qna opening).** ALIGNED. Independently validated by the entire agent-CLI proxy ecosystem [51][52][53]: nobody routes the hosting REPL session; everything interposes below it. The qna's conclusion that Tier-B "/research → Antigravity" means routed *delegation* through a transport, not session switching, matches practice.
- **`RouteQuery`/`RouteDecision`/`TransportRouter`/`StaticRouter` (Part 1 §4).** ALIGNED. RouteLLM's own architecture is an abstract `Router` with a single required method, a router-class registry dict, and a `random` baseline among five shipped routers [6] — structurally identical to `TransportRouter.route()` with `StaticRouter` as the degenerate implementation. The **metadata-only `RouteQuery`** (no prompt text) is DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED: even the surveys' cheapest learned class requires per-request embedding/classifier inference over prompt text [1][2]; Splock's design sits below the entire surveyed taxonomy at O(rules) dict work. The trade is real and should be recorded: it structurally forecloses semantic/learned routing (retrofitting prompt-aware routing means changing a frozen dataclass at three DI seams) — a deliberate commitment, not an oversight.
- **Family-only decision; catalog resolves model within family (Part 1 §4, "Critically…").** ALIGNED — this is the design's strongest call. Providers are absorbing the intra-family tier natively (GPT-5's real-time router [69][73][74]; OpenRouter's zero-fee Auto Router [57]); cross-family *policy* routing (verifier pins, subjects, sealed-path governance) is the tier they structurally cannot own. LiteLLM's alias-vs-deployment split [36] and OpenRouter's model-vs-provider axes [56] are the same two-level separation.
- **Keyed registry, not env-sniffing singleton (Part 1 §1).** ALIGNED as a pattern (RouteLLM's `ROUTER_CLS` dict; LLMRouter's `MetaRouter` auto-discovery [44]). The registry superficially duplicates aisuite/any-llm-class provider registries — the one place a REINVENTION RISK charge could be levelled — but it is DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED: both libraries wrap HTTP APIs with API-key auth; neither supports subscription-authenticated CLI harnesses, an agent-spawn tier (frontmatter-derived agent definitions, hooks, cwd), or per-dialect schema sanitizers. A ~20-line in-repo dict avoids a dependency covering a minority of the transport surface. (Note: Mozilla's claim that aisuite is unmaintained [55] is stale — the repo shows a 2026-06-11 release — but the auth/spawn mismatch alone is decisive.)
- **`capabilities()` gating (Part 1 §3).** ALIGNED — this is precisely OpenRouter's `require_parameters` ("Only use providers that support all parameters in your request") [56] and LiteLLM's pre-call checks (context-window and region filtering) [36]. Two caveats: (i) the qna names no governance mechanism for the capability-tag vocabulary — apply the repo's closed-enum idiom (`bin/_intent/refusal.py`); (ii) LiteLLM filters on *numeric* facts (context window) as well as set membership — whether the frozenset needs a numeric tier is an open design question for /plan. Also see §5: the `"structured-output"` tag is decaying to uniformly-true across families, which fails *safe* (over-constrains visibly rather than mis-routing silently) — keep it, but know its lifespan.
- **`host` vs `family` split (Part 1 §2).** ALIGNED with OpenRouter's two independent axes (models vs providers serving them) [56]. The `register.py:402` hostname-collision hazard the qna flags is repo hygiene with no external analog needed.
- **Ordered first-match-wins rules with three-layer settings precedence (Part 1 §5).** ALIGNED — this is exactly the semantics of Portkey's conditional routing [60]: sequential evaluation where order matters ("place more specific conditions before more general ones," per the docs), a `default` route when all conditions fail, and — direct quote — "If a referenced key is missing… the condition evaluates to `false`; it does not throw an error." **Two schema gaps to close in the reserved `routing_rules_v1.schema.json`:** (a) pin missing-field semantics (Portkey's evaluate-false/skip is the sane default); (b) state whether match fields compose as implicit AND only. **One genuine gap vs the state of the art:** no reserved affordance for fallback or weighted-split semantics. LiteLLM ships `order`, `weight`, and `enable_weighted_failover`; OpenRouter ships an opt-in priority-ordered `models` fallback array distinct from automatic provider failover [59]. The qna's `{match, family, rule_id}` returns exactly one family with no alternates — for Tier-A calls where a family's CLI may be absent/unauthenticated, reserving an optional `fallback_families` (ranked tuple) now costs nothing and avoids a v2 bump. OpenRouter's precedent supports making it *opt-in*, not default.
- **Frontmatter `metadata` for skills; roster for agents; nothing for commands (Part 3).** DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED. The static-facts-on-the-unit / dynamic-rules-central split is structurally LiteLLM tag routing (`tags` in `litellm_params` + central `enable_tag_filtering` + a `default` tag) [37]. The Agent Skills spec confirms `metadata` as the official extension point ("A map from string keys to string values… Clients can use this to store additional properties not defined by the Agent Skills spec," with uniquely-prefixed key names recommended) [72]; the spec's `compatibility` field (1–500 chars, prose environment requirements, "Most skills do not need" it per the spec) is not machine-matchable — the plan should record *why* it was not used. The qna's one-skill-first `--strict` empirical gate remains mandatory (validator acceptance of `metadata` under `--strict` is still unverified locally, per qna evidence 14).
- **Roster pins + fixed-verifier invariant (Part 3).** ALIGNED on structure; DIVERGENT-JUSTIFIED on the invariant, with a **two-sided evidence base the plan should cite accurately**: same-model self-preference bias is real and correlates with self-recognition (NeurIPS 2024 oral [22]; quantified in [23]), *but* the 2025 follow-up [24] finds much self-preference by stronger judges "aligns with objectively superior performance" — so justify the fixed-verifier invariant on the risk of *harmful* self-preference plus reproducibility/pin-stability grounds, not a blanket "self-preference = bias" claim. Residual caveat: the literature measures same-*model* bias; Splock's same-*family* verifier regime (Haiku verifying Opus) is unmeasured, and whether `routing_rules_v1` should reserve cross-family verification remains open. No commercial gateway has an equivalent invariant — this is Splock-differentiating policy.
- **`subjects` + routing-forensics fields (Part 2).** ALIGNED. Subjects-as-governed-vocabulary is the zero-model degenerate case of preference/policy-aligned routing (Arch-Router's user-defined domain-action policies [8]; Select-then-Route's taxonomy-guided selector, EMNLP 2025 industry [25]). The `rule_id`/`model_family`/`routing_rule_id` forensics chain matches gateway provenance norms (OpenRouter's response `model` field showing what was actually used [58]; LiteLLM's `_hidden_params['model_id']` [36]) and anticipates the route-receipts proposal [21]. Leaving `KIND` alone and deferring the orchestrator `agent_assignment` schema bump are both correct sequencing.

**Overall reinvention-risk verdict: low.** Nothing in the qna rebuilds a wheel that exists in adoptable form — the existing wheels are either the wrong problem (strong/weak cost arbitrage, free-text intent inference), the wrong layer (HTTP proxies/gateways), or the wrong health (RouteLLM). The design's few gaps are schema fine print (missing-key semantics, AND composition, fallback reservation, tag governance, staleness dating — see §5), not architecture.

## 5. Blindspots & Obsolescence Risks

The central finding: **provider-native features are invalidating the assumptions of 2024-era routing tools faster than those tools adapt — and Splock's Phase 0 design is already on the survivable side of every documented line, provided it adds a staleness-dating discipline.**

**(a) Native prompt/context caching breaks stateless per-request cost models.** Anthropic's cache pricing spans ~a 10x-20x input-side spread (cache reads 0.1x base; 5-min writes 1.25x; 1-hour writes 2x; free refresh on use; hierarchical invalidation `tools`→`system`→`messages`) [61] — so a mid-workflow family switch can cost ~10x on the input side, and any router pricing calls statelessly is wrong by an order of magnitude. OpenAI changed its own cache economics *between model generations* (writes free before the GPT-5.6 family; 1.25x on 5.6+ with mandatory `prompt_cache_key`; retention 24 h → 30 m) [62] — a router built in 2025 assuming "OpenAI cache writes are free" is already wrong. Gemini adds a cost dimension classic routers don't model at all: per-hour cache *storage rent* alongside 90% cached-input discounts [63]. Industry has consequently converged on cache-affinity/sticky routing: OpenRouter pins model+provider per session explicitly for cache hits [57]; Red Hat's llm-d reports 87.4% cache-hit rates and 88%-faster TTFT from prefix-cache-aware affinity vs cache-blind round-robin [79]; TrueFoundry reports 57x TTFT gains [80]; LiteLLM *declined* character-level prefix routing (closed, not planned) [42]. **Implication:** the qna's granularity — routing once per role/phase/spawn, holding family sticky for the workflow — is the modern correct unit; a "smarter" per-message router would be an anti-pattern.

**(b) Native structured outputs erased a former routing criterion.** All three target families now guarantee schema-conforming output natively: OpenAI (Aug 2024), Gemini (`responseSchema`), and Anthropic — public beta 2025-11-14, **GA 2026-02-04** across the current Claude line, with the API surface already migrating (`output_format` → `output_config.format`) [64]. "Which model can emit valid JSON" is dead as a routing criterion; only schema-*dialect* differences survive. The durable engineering is the recon's per-transport `sanitize_schema()` seam (recon §2.D) — and the parameter rename is a live example of exactly the churn that seam must absorb. The GA event itself — beta framing went stale inside one research pass — is a live demo of this section's decay rate.

**(c) Price shocks and batch tiers invalidate trained cost-quality routers.** Repricing is step-function and overnight: o3 dropped 80% in one day (2025-06-10) [76]; GPT-5 launched at half GPT-4o's input price [77]; and volatility cuts both ways — current OpenAI pricing shows flagship pricing moved *up* ~4x within a generation of GPT-5's launch price [65]. (A community claim of a further ~50% GPT-5 input cut is UNVERIFIED — its source is dead [91] — and is excluded.) Batch tiers are a flat 50% off at all three vendors, combinable (best-effort) with caching at Anthropic (100,000 requests / 256 MB per batch — the 2024 launch's 10k limit is itself stale [66][67]) [65][63]. Workload *shape*, not model choice, is thus the dominant cost lever for latency-insensitive work — and Splock's `intent_kind` (`chain_overnight` vs `interactive`) is already the right routing input for it. The concrete "stale tool" exhibit: RouteLLM's shipped routers are trained on 2024 Chatbot Arena battles for a `gpt-4-1106-preview` vs `mixtral-8x7b` pair — both anchors now multiple generations and repricings obsolete — with no maintenance since 2024-08-10 [6]. The 2026 literature itself documents the fragility: routers "systematically default to the most capable and most expensive model even when cheaper models already suffice" because small prediction errors flip discrete orderings [26]; RouterArena ships an automated leaderboard-update framework precisely because router evaluations go stale [14]; router evaluation methodology is still being re-litigated [27][28][29].

**(d) Provider-native auto-routing absorbs the intra-family problem.** GPT-5's launch-day router crisis [73][74] and OpenRouter's zero-fee NotDiamond Auto Router [57] jointly establish that per-prompt difficulty/quality routing is (i) hard even with full telemetry and (ii) already a free commodity. Splock building one would compete with a commodity while its actual differentiators — policy, roles, verifier independence, subjects, sealed-path governance — are unserved by any provider or gateway.

**(e) Capability convergence shrinks the arbitrage.** Anthropic's own framing: five months after Sonnet 4 was state-of-the-art, Haiku 4.5 offers "similar levels of coding performance" at one-third the cost and 2x+ speed [68]. Community trackers (lower authority, methodology unverified — flagged) show the top five frontier models within ~10–25 Elo, "approximately interchangeable on quality at the top" per the tracker [85][86]. The strong-vs-weak spread a learned router monetizes decays to near-zero within one Splock release cycle; what durably differs across vendors is capabilities, latency profiles, tooling, and terms — exactly what `capabilities()` tags and PORTABILITY_REVIEW's platform matrix encode.

**Future-proofing guidance for Splock (the durable split):**

- **Durably client-side (Splock owns):** cross-family policy routing (no provider routes to a competitor; OpenRouter does, but is itself a fourth vendor with no notion of Splock's roles); the verifier-independence invariant and fixed pins; subject/data-governance affinity ("cybersecurity → Codex" is a policy fact no provider can infer); workload-shape selection (interactive vs batch-eligible overnight chains); forensics (`rule_id`, `resolved_model`, observed `cost_usd`).
- **Delegate to providers/commodity:** intra-family model selection (catalog + provider aliases); per-prompt difficulty classification; cache-management mechanics (automatic at OpenAI/Gemini; breakpoint placement is a transport concern at Anthropic); structured-output enforcement (only dialect sanitization stays client-side).
- **Keep rules declarative and data-dated so staleness is detectable, not silent:** (i) an `as_of` ISO-date field on every rule and on the subjects vocabulary, with a conformance-test staleness warning; (ii) *no numeric prices or quality scores in rules, ever* — if cost ever matters, reference a separate dated price-snapshot file; (iii) record `resolved_model` (concrete ID, never alias) and observed `cost_usd` so assumed-vs-observed drift is queryable; (iv) route once per phase/spawn, hold family sticky (cache affinity); (v) capability tags are the only "model knowledge" permitted in rules — they decay slowest, and they decay to uniformly-true, which fails safe (an obsolete `requires` tag over-constrains visibly rather than mis-routing silently).

## Recommendations for /plan

Additive changes to the Phase 0 plan substrate. The qna's existing recommendations 1–6 survive this research structurally unchanged; the following refine or extend them. No plan file is edited here.

1. **Adopt "model after, never import" as explicit policy.** LiteLLM's in-process `Router` is the design reference (filter-then-select, alias-keyed registry, single strategy-ABC seam, always-valid default) [36]; no routing-library dependency ships in Phase 0 or later — the import-cost and supply-chain evidence ([39][40], CVE-2026-42208 [35]) should be cited in the plan as the rationale.
2. **Specify the `TransportRouter` contract as two testable stages:** capability/eligibility filter (`required_capabilities <= transport.capabilities()`) as a distinct stage *before* rule matching, per LiteLLM's pre-call-check pattern. Acceptance: each stage unit-testable in isolation.
3. **Harden the reserved `routing_rules_v1.schema.json` with four decisions now (documented, not implemented):** (a) missing-key semantics = evaluate-false/skip, never error (Portkey precedent [60]); (b) match fields compose as implicit AND only in v1; (c) reserve an *optional* `fallback_families` ranked tuple (opt-in, per OpenRouter's model-fallbacks precedent [59]) so a family's absent/unauthenticated CLI has defined semantics without a v2 bump; (d) shape rules as named policies with decoupled model targets, per the Arch-Router paper's config schema (the paper's schema, not its LM matcher) [8].
4. **Add the staleness-dating discipline (new, this research's main addition):** require an `as_of` ISO-date field on every routing rule and on the subjects vocabulary; add a conformance-test warning when `as_of` exceeds a configurable age; **forbid numeric prices or quality scores in rules** — if cost ever enters, it must reference a separate dated price-snapshot file (§5 evidence: [61][62][63][65][76][77]).
5. **Specify stickiness semantics:** document that routing occurs only at spawn seams and the family decision is held sticky per phase/spawn (cache-affinity rationale [57][79]); decide whether `RouteDecision` reserves an explicit `sticky_scope` (phase | slug | session) field now or records stickiness as implied — either way, write it down in Phase 0.
6. **Capability-tag vocabulary governance:** define the tags as a closed enum per the `bin/_intent/refusal.py` idiom; align tag names with public taxonomies (OpenRouter `supported_parameters`, LiteLLM `supports_*`) where semantics overlap; note in the plan that `"structured-output"` is expected to become uniformly-true (fails safe — retain it) [64]. Flag as an open design question whether a numeric tier (max context/output tokens) is needed alongside set membership.
7. **Forensics/receipts:** keep `RouteDecision.rule_id` + `reason` and the intent-registry `model_family`/`routing_rule_id` fields; record `resolved_model` as a concrete ID (never an alias) and observed `cost_usd` for assumed-vs-observed drift detection; consider a minimal structured shape for `reason` (route-receipt inspiration [21] — a preprint, so treat as inspiration, not a standard). Deliberately *do not* add a `confidence`/`score` field in Phase 0 — flagged as scope-creep risk; revisit in the `dynamic_model_routing` slug.
8. **Verifier-invariant citation hygiene:** when the plan justifies the fixed-verifier catalog contract, cite the self-preference literature accurately as two-sided ([22][23] vs [24]) — ground the invariant in harmful-self-preference risk plus reproducibility, and record that same-*family* (cross-model) verification bias is an unmeasured regime and cross-family verification remains an open reservation.
9. **Documentation nits:** record why the Agent Skills `compatibility` field was not used for routing hints (prose, not machine-matchable [72]); keep the one-skill-first `--strict` rollout gate (qna G8) and the metadata-survival-at-import check (qna G9) as mandatory verification tasks.
10. **Open questions to carry into the `dynamic_model_routing` slug:** subscription-CLI cache/cost telemetry visibility in `_sdk_bridge` payloads (drift detection under subscription auth); Codex/Antigravity caching semantics under subscription auth; whether grown `subjects` vocabularies ever justify Arch-Router-style natural-language matching (currently out of scope). Standing references to bookmark there: Awesome-Routing-LLMs [33] and the RouterArena live leaderboard [14] (first systematic source of measured per-router routing latency).
